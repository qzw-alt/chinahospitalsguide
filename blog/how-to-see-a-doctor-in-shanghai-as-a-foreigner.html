How to See a Doctor in Shanghai as a Foreigner

Shanghai is one of the easiest cities in mainland China for foreigners to navigate medically, but it still helps to know when to use a public hospital, when to pay for a private clinic, and what to prepare before you go.

Quick answer: for routine care or easier English support, many foreigners in Shanghai start with a private international clinic or hospital. For complex specialist care, advanced surgery, or top academic departments, public hospitals are often the stronger option. In emergencies, go to the emergency department or call 120.

Medical disclaimer: this page is general information, not a diagnosis or treatment advice. If you have urgent symptoms such as chest pain, breathing difficulty, stroke symptoms, severe bleeding, loss of consciousness, or serious injury, seek emergency care immediately.

1. What Makes Shanghai Easier for Foreigners

Compared with many other mainland cities, Shanghai offers more English-speaking clinics, more international departments, and a broader mix of public tertiary hospitals plus private care options. That makes it a common first stop for expats, business travelers, international students, and families living in China.

  • More international patient infrastructure than most cities.
  • More hospitals familiar with direct billing and international insurance questions.
  • A dense concentration of strong public hospitals for cardiology, neurology, orthopedics, maternity, general medicine, and specialty surgery.

2. Public vs Private in Shanghai

3. Good Starting Points by Need

Based on the hospitals already listed in the site directory, these are the kinds of Shanghai facilities foreigners often shortlist first:

  • General medicine and broad specialist access: Ruijin Hospital, Renji Hospital, Zhongshan Hospital, Changhai Hospital.
  • Neurology and neurosurgery: Huashan Hospital.
  • Orthopedics and sports medicine: Shanghai Sixth Hospital.
  • Maternity and obstetrics: Fudan University Women's Hospital.
  • Plastic or reconstructive surgery: Shanghai Ninth Hospital.
  • Traditional Chinese Medicine: Longhua Hospital, Shuguang Hospital.
Practical tip: do not choose only by reputation. The right hospital also depends on your exact department, urgency, language comfort, insurance, and whether you need an international service desk.

4. How Registration Usually Works

In Shanghai public hospitals, the process often looks like this:

  1. Choose the right department before you arrive.
  2. Register with your passport or patient card.
  3. Pay the consultation fee.
  4. See the doctor and receive orders for tests, imaging, prescriptions, or referral.
  5. Pay for each ordered service unless the hospital follows a different billing sequence.

Some hospitals offer WeChat, app, kiosk, or international desk registration. For foreigners, passport-based registration can still be slower than for local patients, so allow extra time.

5. Insurance and Payment in Shanghai

Shanghai is one of the better cities for international insurance handling, especially in private hospitals and some international departments, but you should still confirm the details before you go.

  • Ask whether direct billing is available.
  • Confirm whether pre-authorization is needed for imaging, surgery, admission, or specialist visits.
  • Keep receipts, invoices, prescriptions, reports, and discharge notes.
  • For public hospitals, be ready to pay first and claim later if necessary.

6. Emergency Care in Shanghai

For emergencies in Shanghai, call 120 or go directly to the nearest emergency department. If you are in a hotel, office, school, or apartment building and your Chinese is limited, ask staff or a neighbor to help explain the situation to emergency services.

  • Carry your passport, allergies, medication list, and emergency contact information.
  • If you have international insurance, contact the assistance hotline as soon as it is practical.
  • Do not delay urgent care because you are still trying to compare hospitals.

7. What To Bring to an Appointment

  • Passport and local phone number if you have one.
  • Insurance card or policy information.
  • Recent medical reports, imaging, lab results, and medication list.
  • A Chinese phrase sheet or translation app.
  • Questions written down in advance, especially if you may need to move quickly through a public hospital visit.

8. Best Strategy for Most Foreigners

If your case is not urgent and you are unsure where to start, a practical Shanghai strategy is:

  1. Use a private international clinic or hospital for a first evaluation if you want easier communication.
  2. Move to a top public tertiary hospital if the issue is clearly complex, surgical, or specialty-heavy.
  3. Ask for written records in English where possible so any follow-up or insurance claim is easier.
