For patients facing kidney failure, end-stage liver disease, or end-stage heart failure, organ transplantation is often the only life-prolonging option. In the US, UK, Canada, and Australia, the wait can stretch from 2 to 7 years for a deceased-donor kidney, and many patients deteriorate — or die — before receiving an organ. China, by contrast, has built one of the highest-volume, fastest-moving transplant systems in the world, with much shorter wait times and end-to-end packages priced 70-85% below Western hospitals.

This pillar guide explains how the system works, what each transplant costs, which hospitals lead in volume and outcomes, and what international patients should know about COTRS allocation and the documentation required to access care in China.

🇨🇳 China's Organ Transplant Scale & the COTRS Voluntary System

China transitioned to a fully voluntary, public-facing organ donation system on January 1, 2015. Allocation for deceased-donor organs is now run by the China Organ Transplant Response System (COTRS), a national computerized registry under the National Health Commission. COTRS matches donors to recipients based on medical urgency, blood/tissue compatibility, waiting time, and geography — a structure functionally similar to UNOS in the United States or Eurotransplant in Europe.

Scale of the Program

  • 10,000+ transplants per year across kidney, liver, heart, lung, and pancreas — making China the second-largest program in the world by annual volume.
  • More than 170 transplant centers are licensed by the National Health Commission.
  • Deceased-donor donation rates have grown substantially since 2015, with annual deceased donations now in the multi-thousand range.
  • China is a member of the WHO and participates in global transplant registries and ethics dialogues.

How COTRS Works

All deceased-donor organs in China flow through COTRS. The system captures donor authorization, organ type, donor characteristics, and the recipient waitlist. When an organ becomes available, COTRS generates a ranked match list and the transplant team confirms acceptance. This is the same principle used by UNOS in the US, Eurotransplant in the EU, and the UK's NHS Blood and Transplant service.

✅ Reassurance for International Patients:

  • All deceased-donor organs in China are sourced from voluntary, publicly documented donations.
  • Allocation is system-driven (COTRS), not by hospital discretion or ability to pay.
  • International patients are placed in the queue under the same medical-urgency rules as Chinese citizens.

⏱️ Why China Has the World's Shortest Transplant Wait Times

Two factors drive China's short transplant wait times: a steadily rising voluntary donation rate and an efficient COTRS-driven allocation pipeline.

1. High Voluntary Donation Rate

China has invested in public donation campaigns, hospital-based donor coordinators, and a national registry for donor authorization. Deceased-donor donation in mainland China has grown substantially since 2015, with annual donations now in the multi-thousand range.

2. Efficient COTRS Allocation

COTRS produces ranked match lists in real time, removing the manual, hospital-by-hospital negotiation that historically lengthened Western wait times. Organs are matched to the highest-urgency compatible recipient across the national network.

3. Concentration of High-Volume Centers

A relatively small number of high-volume centers perform a large share of the country's transplants, so they have consistent access to donor flow and well-tuned surgical teams. A patient at one of these centers typically has a realistic transplant window measured in weeks to a few months — not years.

📊 Realistic Wait Estimates (2026, top Chinese centers):

  • Kidney: Weeks to a few months for compatible blood-type match
  • Liver: Often weeks, especially for status-1 (high-urgency) candidates
  • Heart: Days to weeks once accepted onto the active list
  • Lung: Weeks to a few months

Individual times depend on blood type, antibody profile, size match, and medical urgency.

🌏 International Patient Access: COTRS Rules & Documents

International patients can access deceased-donor transplant in China, but the path is more structured than a typical elective procedure. Below is what you should know.

Eligibility Rules

  • Deceased-donor only. Living-donor transplantation is not available to foreign recipients. Organs come from voluntary deceased donation through COTRS.
  • Medical urgency counts. Your clinical priority is determined by your medical record and entered into COTRS the same way a Chinese citizen's would be.
  • Sponsoring center required. A licensed Chinese transplant center must agree to list you. Most centers will review your case remotely before issuing an invitation.
  • No organ purchasing. Allocation is medically driven. Patients pay for medical services, not for the organ itself — the same principle codified by the WHO and followed in the US, EU, and elsewhere.

Documents Typically Required

1

Complete Medical Records (English)

Diagnosis, pathology, imaging (CT/MRI/ultrasound), lab work, prior treatment summary, current medication list. The hospital's international team will specify additional records on a case-by-case basis.

2

Recent Specialist Letter(s)

A letter from your home-country transplant nephrologist/hepatologist/cardiologist confirming diagnosis and that transplant is indicated.

3

Cross-Match & Tissue Typing

Most centers will request HLA typing, panel reactive antibody (PRA) testing for kidney candidates, and blood-group confirmation before listing.

4

Passport & Visa Documentation

The hospital will issue a visa invitation letter for an M-visa (medical visa). Hainan and several other regions also offer visa-on-arrival options for medical travelers. See our China medical visa guide for details.

5

Financial Guarantee

Wire-transfer deposit or insurance pre-authorization for the surgery package. The hospital international office provides a detailed cost breakdown up front.

English Coordinators

All the hospitals named above maintain dedicated international patient offices with English-speaking coordinators. Many have additional capacity in Arabic, Russian, French, and other languages, reflecting the global patient mix. For the practical mechanics of the trip, our how to prepare for medical travel to China article covers flights, hotels, and recovery logistics.

⚠️ Plan for an Open-Ended Stay: Once listed, you may be asked to remain in-country (or nearby) so the team can bring you in for transplant within hours of an organ offer. Most centers help families arrange extended-stay apartments near the hospital.

📊 Quality, Outcomes & Survival Rates

Volume alone does not define a transplant center — outcomes do. Top Chinese centers publish outcomes in line with international benchmarks.

Kidney Transplant Outcomes

  • 1-year graft survival: 90%+ at high-volume centers
  • 5-year graft survival: 70-80% range
  • Source: Chinese Scientific Registry of Kidney Transplantation and published single-center series

Liver Transplant Outcomes

  • 1-year patient survival: 80-90% at high-volume centers (varies by indication)
  • 5-year patient survival: 60-75% range, with hepatocellular carcinoma cases tracked separately
  • Source: China Liver Transplant Registry (CLTR) and individual center data

Heart & Lung Outcomes

  • Heart: 1-year survival in the 80%+ range; 5-year survival in the 65-75% range at experienced centers
  • Lung: Improving steadily; 1-year survival 70%+ at the highest-volume programs

Quality & Safety Standards

✅ Quality Assurance:

  • National licensing: All transplant centers are inspected and re-licensed periodically by the National Health Commission
  • Donor screening: Strict infectious-disease screening (HIV, hepatitis B/C, syphilis, others) on every deceased donor
  • Multidisciplinary teams: Transplant surgery, transplant nephrology/hepatology/cardiology, infectious disease, pathology, ICU, and transplant pharmacy under one roof
  • Hospital infection control: Comparable to Western academic centers; published SSI and CLABSI rates align with international norms

❓ Frequently Asked Questions

How much does an organ transplant cost in China in 2026?

Kidney transplant: ~$70,000. Liver transplant: $60,000-$100,000. Heart transplant: $80,000-$120,000. Lung and pancreas are similarly 70-85% below US list prices. Packages include pre-op workup, surgery, ICU/ward stay, 30-day immunosuppressant supply, and 90 days of in-person follow-up. International airfare, long-term immunosuppression, and readmissions are billed separately.

How long is the wait for an organ transplant in China?

Weeks to a few months for most organ types — a fraction of the multi-year waits common in the US, UK, Canada, and Australia. China has one of the world's shortest transplant wait times due to a high voluntary donation rate and an efficient COTRS allocation system in place since 2015. Individual times depend on blood type, antibody profile, size match, and medical urgency.

Can international patients receive organ transplants in China?

Yes, but eligibility is determined case-by-case under COTRS rules. Patients typically need a confirmed diagnosis, a sponsoring transplant center, complete medical records in English, and to coordinate with the hospital's international department. Living-donor transplantation is not available to foreign recipients — only deceased-donor allocation through COTRS.

What are the survival rates for organ transplants in China?

Outcomes at top Chinese transplant centers track international benchmarks. One-year graft survival for kidney transplant is typically above 90%, with 5-year survival above 70%. Liver transplant 1-year patient survival is generally in the 80-90% range at high-volume centers. Published registry data from the Chinese Scientific Registry of Kidney Transplantation and the China Liver Transplant Registry (CLTR) underpin these figures.
