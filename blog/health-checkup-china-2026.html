8. What to Expect: The Health Checkup Process

Before Your Visit (Preparation Checklist):

Preparation Checklist

  • Fast for 10-12 hours (water allowed, no food, coffee, or gum)
  • Avoid alcohol for 48 hours before
  • No strenuous exercise 24 hours prior
  • Bring previous medical records and imaging CDs
  • List all current medications and supplements
  • Women: Schedule outside menstrual period (day 3-10 ideal)
  • Wear comfortable, easily removable clothing (no metal)
  • Leave valuables at hotel
  • Bring passport for registration

Day-of Process Timeline:

  1. 7:30 AM - Registration (30 min): International desk check-in, medical history questionnaire, wristband placement
  2. 8:00 AM - Blood Draw (15 min): Fasting blood panel, multiple tubes collected, hydration break offered
  3. 8:30 AM - Imaging Block (90 min):
    • Chest X-ray or CT
    • Abdominal ultrasound
    • Thyroid ultrasound (if included)
    • Cardiac ultrasound/ECHO (if included)
  4. 10:00 AM - Breakfast Break: Hospitals provide healthy breakfast for fasting patients
  5. 10:30 AM - Functional Tests (45 min): ECG, pulmonary function, bone density, vision, hearing
  6. 11:15 AM - Specialist Consultations (60 min): Physical exam, discussion of symptoms, risk factor review
  7. 12:15 PM - Endoscopy Slot (if selected): Gastroscopy/colonoscopy with sedation (2-3 hours including recovery)
  8. 2:00 PM - Wrap-up: Discharge instructions, report timeline, follow-up scheduling

Timeline by Package Type:

  • Basic Package: 4-5 hours, same-day results for lab/imaging
  • Comprehensive Package: 6-8 hours, preliminary results same day, final report in 3-5 days
  • VIP Package with Endoscopy: Full day (2 sessions if combined), comprehensive report in 5-7 days

9. Risks and Important Considerations

⚠️ Medical Risks to Understand

  • Radiation Exposure: CT scans involve ionizing radiation. A chest CT delivers ~2-3 mSv (equivalent to 1 year of natural background radiation). Discuss whether low-dose protocols are used.
  • False Positives: Tumor markers can elevate due to benign conditions. CA19-9 rises with gallstones; CEA with smoking. Always confirm with imaging or biopsy.
  • Incidental Findings: 15-20% of CT/MRI scans reveal unexpected findings requiring further investigation. This can cause anxiety and additional costs.
  • Endoscopy Risks: Perforation risk is very low (1:10,000) but real. Bleeding can occur after polypectomy. Sedation risks for patients with severe cardiac/pulmonary disease.
  • Overdiagnosis: Some cancers found on screening (especially thyroid, prostate) may be indolent and never require treatment. This is the "thyroid cancer epidemic" dilemma.

⚡ Practical Considerations for International Patients

  • Language: Verify English proficiency at international desk. For complex packages, consider hiring a medical interpreter ($100-200/day)
  • Medical Records: Request English reports. Ask for DICOM digital imaging files to share with home physicians.
  • Follow-up Plans: Discuss any findings with your home doctor before travel. Establish a local follow-up protocol.
  • Insurance: Confirm whether your insurer covers international preventive care. Save all receipts for reimbursement.
  • Timeline: Allow 3-7 days in China for comprehensive screening. Some results require pathology processing time.
  • Continuity of Care: China hospitals do not replace ongoing care. Establish a relationship with a local physician for continuity.
  • Payment: Most hospitals require upfront payment. International credit cards accepted but expect 2-3% foreign transaction fee.

Who Should Consider VIP/Comprehensive Packages?

  • Age 45+ with no recent comprehensive checkup
  • Family history of cancer, cardiovascular disease, or diabetes
  • Personal history of smoking (past or present)
  • Symptoms: unexplained weight loss, fatigue, pain, or bleeding
  • Occupational exposure to toxins or carcinogens
  • Those seeking peace of mind about current health status

Who Might Prefer Basic Screening?

  • Young adults (25-40) with no symptoms or family history
  • Those with recent checkups at home (within 2 years)
  • Limited budget prioritizing specific concerns
  • First-tier screening for asymptomatic individuals
